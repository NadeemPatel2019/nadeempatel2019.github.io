College life after military: the mental health challenges, the transition, and the help

  • December 5, 2019
  • By Nadeem Patel, Jimmy Yook

After serving in the military, individuals transition to civilian life by getting a job and spending time with family. Others decide to go back to school for higher education. However, there are also veterans who come back with uncertainty or developed mental conditions after serving. Northwestern University looks to make the transition easier as student veterans seek help for those conditions along with the challenges of college.

According to Veterans Affairs Office of Research and Development, some of the mental conditions associated with veterans include:
Post-traumatic disorder
Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) is a mental health condition that's triggered by a terrifying event — either experiencing it or witnessing it. Symptoms may include flashbacks, nightmares and severe anxiety, as well as uncontrollable thoughts about the event.
Depression
Depression is a mood disorder that causes a persistent feeling of sadness and loss of interest. Also called major depressive disorder or clinical depression, it affects how you feel, think and behave and can lead to a variety of emotional and physical problems. You may have trouble doing normal day-to-day activities, and sometimes you may feel as if life isn't worth living.
Traumatic Brain Injury
Traumatic brain injury usually results from a violent blow or jolt to the head or body. An object that penetrates brain tissue, such as a bullet or shattered piece of skull, also can cause traumatic brain injury.
Schizophrenia
Schizophrenia is a serious mental disorder in which people interpret reality abnormally. Schizophrenia may result in some combination of hallucinations, delusions, and extremely disordered thinking and behavior that impairs daily functioning, and can be disabling.
Bipolar Disorder
Bipolar disorder, formerly called manic depression, is a mental health condition that causes extreme mood swings that include emotional highs (mania or hypomania) and lows (depression).
Source: Mayo Clinic

The U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs released its 2019 National Veteran Suicide Prevention Report, which states that the suicide rate is 17-per-day among veterans according to its most recent data from 2017. The conditions associated with veterans, including post-traumatic stress disorder, depression and traumatic brain injury, are a few of the major contributors to the high suicide rate. In order to help veterans deal with mental conditions, Veterans Affairs provides services through VA health care. The services include counseling, therapy, medication or a combination of the options. However, veterans that decide to go to college may not necessarily have easy access to those services.

According to US News, nearly 1 million veterans are enrolled in higher education under the Forever GI Bill, but less than 10,000 are enrolled in selective colleges and universities. Northwestern University, a private institution, has reportedly seen its acceptance rate rise for the first time in 10 years to 8.9 percent for the class of 2023. Part of this increase has also been the rise of enrolled veterans. Since the university’s commitment to improving veterans enrollment in 2016, the number of enrolled veterans has gone up by 38 from 239 to 277 in 2018.

While the number of students accepted has increased, the number of individuals seeking clinical support services has risen as well. Over the past ten years, Counseling and Psychological Services has seen a 50 percent increase in utilization of services, The Daily Northwestern reported. In 2018, around 3,200 students seeked primary mental health service on campus, including more than 1,000 urgent care appointments. In order to deal with the deamnd, CAPS aimed to hire two new staff members to provide the necessary service and suicide screenings according to Vice President for Student Affairs Patricia Telles-Irvin. CAPS is also one of the few mental health services available to Northwestern veterans. However, the Student Veterans’ Resources page will simply link the individual to the CAPS homepage. This means there is no specific portion of CAPS dedicated to veterans, and they are in the same pool as the rest of the Northwestern community looking to use CAPS services.

According to CAPS, the average wait time to see a therapist is about a week after a brief phone consultation. However, the “How to Make an Appointment” page indicates that the Evanston CAPS office has reached full capacity for the current Fall quarter, so the next available appointment is not until Winter quarter.

In fall 2016, the Veteran Support Initiative was launched to enhance faculty and staff support, student financial aid, veteran recognition and student support services at Northwestern. According to the Office of Institutional Diversity and Inclusion, the recommendation to improve support services is being “reviewed, prioritized and explored for further implementation.” At the moment, it is not clear how much Northwestern has truly improved on providing mental health services.

While it is not associated with CAPS or the 2016 initiative, The Family Institute at Northwestern University is another option for veterans. Through its partnership with Northwestern’s Graduate School and Office of Research, the institute houses The Center for Applied Psychological and Family Studies. Dr. Jonathan Sutton, the director of Cognitive Behavioral Therapies Program at The Family Institute, states that he has worked with veterans returning to school, work or both. A major part of working with such veterans is helping them with their transition process, which can be stressful and difficult to handle. According to a survey by the VA, two-thirds of veterans reported that adjusting to civilian life is difficult and are not sure how to get help. The anxiety and stress that comes from such uncertainty can contribute to mental health issues.

If there are emotional components to what you’re dealing with, whether it is trauma, adjustment to civilian life, depression or anxiety, they are important to address.

- Jonathan Sutton, Ph.D., Director of the Cognitive Behavioral Therapies Program at The Family Institute at Northwestern University

“The question is what are some of the typical concerns? One of them is just the adjustment,” says Sutton about the type of help many veterans seek. “If there are emotional components to what you’re dealing with, whether it is trauma, adjustment to civilian life, depression or anxiety, they are important to address because they do take up some bandwidth when you’re also trying to address other things like family or school.”

Sutton said veterans can also find help at one of the three major veteran medical centers in the Chicagoland area, including Captain James A. Lovell Federal Health Care Center, Jesse Brown VA Medical Center and Hines VA Medical Center. According to Sutton, the range of ages at such clinics can be from young veterans that are just returning home to veterans that served in World War II, Korea and Vietnam.

While Northwestern may still be in the process of improving the needs of veterans, Sutton says seeking out the available resources is crucial.

“If they don’t know it already, whether it is somebody on campus who’s knowledgeable, a veteran service organizations or going online and finding out some of the benefits for education and health care, says Sutton about utilizing resources. “Those could be very useful things for help.”

Veterans can also call the widely used crisis line at 1-800-273-TALK and press one for a separate tree dedicated for veterans in crisis.
